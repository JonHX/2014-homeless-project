What is homelessness?
In a broad sense the law defines somebody as being homeless if they do not have a legal right to occupy any accommodation, or if their accommodation is unsuitable to live in. This can include a large range of circumstances, including but not restricted to, the following:
  • having no accommodation at all
  • having accommodation that is not reasonable to live in, even in the short-term (eg violence or health reasons)
  • having a legal right to accommodation that you cannot access (eg if you have been illegally evicted)
  • living in accommodation you have no legal right to occupy (eg temporarily staying with friends)
Unfortunately many people only associate homelessness with sleeping rough, but this ignores the range and scale of the current problem. The reality is that the majority of homeless people are families or single people who are not ‘sleeping rough’. Many may be staying with friends and relations on a temporary basis. Whilst others live in temporary accommodation, such as hostels, bed and breakfast hotels, refuges and sometimes night shelters. For many, this means living standards are poor, that is detrimental to their health and well-being."Homelessness means loss, loss, loss... It is not just the loss of a home, maybe of a partner or of family life, of supportive friends or of a known community. It involves the loss of confidence and self-esteem. The loss of opportunities. These losses are less obvious... and the long-term effects on children in particular, and the stigma of homelessness, are not ever really taken on board. It’s not just the reasons why people become homeless that are important but what it does to you." - Health Worker

The Statistics
Currently in England there are:
13140 households accept as homeless.
Definition
These are households where the applicable council has found to be homeless. They are not all accepted as being owed a duty by the council.
4850 households found to be homeless but not in priority need.
Definition
These are households accepted as homeless but found not to be in priority need by the local council. They usually wait a substanially extended time for rehousing.
2050 households found to be 'intentionally homeless'.
Definition
Being intentionally homeless means that you are homeless because you left accommodation that you could have stayed in or a deliberate action create homelessness. E.g Not paying rent when you can afford it.
8780 families with children accepted as homeless.
Definition
Households with children accepted as homeless by the local council. These households are usually priority due to effects of homelessness on children.
This equates to a massive 28820 homeless households in england.

What causes homelessness?

Sadly, many people view homelessness as the result of personal failings, and consider that if the economy is going well, there is no excuse for being financially stable.

But this belief is not justtified by the facts, which show that homelessness is caused by an array of complex interplay between a person's individual circumstances and adverse 'structural' factors not within their direct control.

The reality is that a number of issues can result in homelessness, both structural and personal.

Personal Causes
  • An array of Individual factors
  • Family background
  • From an institutional background
Structural Causes
  • Unemployment
  • Poverty
  • Lack of affordable housing
  • Structuring of Government policies
"The average person is just two missed pay checks away from being homeless."
With the average person only two missed pay checks away from starting a process which could leave them homeless and over 80,000 children waking up in temporary accomodation each day due to financial restraints, it's no wonder it is the most common factor of homelessness.

Although it can also be solely finanicial issues, factors such as lack of social support, debts – especially mortgage repayments, lack of affordable housing and rent arrears. This cause of homelessness can also stem from lack of qualifications, poor physical and mental health, relationship breakdown and crime involvement resulting in unemployment.
"Our upbringing plays a very critical part of our future, how we manage ourselves and how the things around us can effect us, and even mental illness. This can effect a persons future stability almost directly."
Homelessness can also occur from family, upbringing and relationships. Family disputes and breakdown can leave people with nowhere to stay as they are no longer welcome in the family home. Also having parents with alcohol or drug problems may leave some youth feeling at risk of abuse at home or they may not be comfortable in that environment.

Upbringing issues such as sexual and physical abuse can not only have immediate risks of homelessness due to feeling unsafe in their current household, but can also have long term mental effects which leave them unable to work and leave them financially homeless.

The most common of these causes are relationship background, when a spouse leaves their partner either with a debt, rent arrears or a big financial burden. This can also create homelessness from a partner not having a legal right to occupy a household and to have nowhere to stay.
"What shocks me is that so many people leave care and become homeless, and when you're homeless you get into crime, prostitution and drugs, and it is a vicious circle. That's what we need to change."
Institutional and armed forces causes are usually more complicated cases, they are very individual problems usually associated with mental and physical conditions. Institutional background could be anyone coming from or growing up within a care home or facility. This also applies to those coming from prison. The armed forces homeless population in London alone is estimated to be between 2,000 and 3,000 by the Ministry of Defence.
"About a quarter of the population will experience some kind of mental health problem in the course of a year, with mixed anxiety and depression the most common mental disorder in Britain"
Physical and Mental illness can also be a caused from being homeless, but many also become homeless from this cause. Unfortunately this can be a vicious circle whilst trying to rehabilitate a homeless patient back into normal society. Thankfully in this country the NHS provides free healthcare to all, but this doesn’t necessarily mean that they can afford prescribed medication and they often leave illness until it becomes in some cases severe.

Mental illness can in many cases lead to a person feeling un-motivated and un-fit to work. When social support isn’t available this can lead to further issues, financial and eventually ending in homelessness. With a quarter of the population estimated to experience a mental health problem within their lifetime, the problem is very relevant.

Those with experience of homelessness are also more likely to have a unhealthy lifestyle, which can result in longterm health problems or worsen existing issues. Despite 90% of those surveyed reporting that they are registered with a GP, a large amount of homeless people have reported that they are not receiving help with their health problems.
